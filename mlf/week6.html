MLF — Singular Value Decomposition & Positive Definiteness

Lecture 1: Singular Value Decomposition

1. Spectral Theorem (Real Symmetric Matrices)

  • Definition: Any real symmetric matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ can be diagonalized as $A = Q \lambda Q^T$.
  • Key Properties:
    • Eigenvalues are real.
    • $Q$ is orthogonal ($Q^T Q = I$).
    • Columns of $Q$ are orthonormal eigenvectors.
    • $\lambda$ is diagonal with eigenvalues.

Example

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ -2 & -2 \end{bmatrix}$$ Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = -3$, $\lambda_2 = 2$
Eigenvectors: $x_1 = [1,2]$, $x_2 = [-2,1]$
Normalized: $q_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}[1,2]$,   $q_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}[-2,1]$

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

  • Definition: For any matrix $A$, $A = Q_1 \Sigma Q_2^T$.
  • Components:
    • $Q_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$: orthogonal.
    • $Q_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$: orthogonal.
    • $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$: diagonal with singular values.

Singular Values

  • Compute $A^T A$ (symmetric, positive semi-definite).
  • Eigenvalues: $\lambda_i \geq 0$.
  • Singular values: $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$, ordered $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$.

Constructing SVD Matrices

  • $Q_2$: eigenvectors of $A^T A$.
  • $Q_1$: first $r$ columns from $y_i = \dfrac{1}{\sigma_i} A x_i$

Important Relationships

$AA^T = Q_1 (\Sigma \Sigma^T) Q_1^T \to$ columns of $Q_1$ are eigenvectors of $AA^T$.
$A^T A = Q_2 (\Sigma^T \Sigma) Q_2^T \to$ columns of $Q_2$ are eigenvectors of $A^T A$.

Spectral vs SVD Comparison

FeatureSpectral TheoremSVD
Matrix TypeSymmetric onlyAny matrix
ShapeSquareRectangular allowed
Decomposition$Q\Lambda Q^T$$Q_1 \Sigma Q_2^T$
ValuesEigenvaluesSingular values

Intuition

SVD = Rotate ($Q_2^T$) → Stretch ($\Sigma$) → Rotate again ($Q_1$).

Applications

  • Dimensionality reduction (PCA)
  • Image compression
  • Noise reduction
  • Recommender systems
  • Solving least squares problems

Lecture 2: Example of SVD

Introduction to SVD

Definition: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) expresses any matrix $A$ as: $A = Q_1 \Sigma Q_2^T$

Components

  • $Q_1$: Left orthogonal matrix ($Q_1^T Q_1 = I$)
  • $\Sigma$: Diagonal matrix containing singular values
  • $Q_2$: Right orthogonal matrix ($Q_2^T Q_2 = I$)

Key Idea

  • Works for any matrix (square, rectangular, non-diagonalizable)
  • More general than eigen-decomposition

Diagonalizability Check (Before SVD)

Condition: A matrix is diagonalizable if it has enough linearly independent eigenvectors.

Special Case:

  • If matrix is symmetric → always diagonalizable
  • Then: SVD $\approx$ Eigen-decomposition

Non-diagonalizable Case:

  • If eigenvectors are insufficient → use SVD

SVD Procedure (Step-by-Step)

Steps

  1. Compute $A^T A$
    • Always symmetric
    • Find eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ and eigenvectors $x_i$
  2. Form $Q_2$ (Right Singular Vectors)
    • Normalize eigenvectors
    • Arrange as columns: $Q_2 = [x_1\ x_2\ \ldots]$
  3. Compute Singular Values: $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$, $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots$
  4. Form $\Sigma$: $\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_2 \end{bmatrix}$
  5. Find $Q_1$ (Left Singular Vectors): $y_i = \dfrac{1}{\sigma_i} A x_i$ — unit, orthogonal vectors. Arrange as columns: $Q_1 = [y_1\ y_2]$
Final Result: $A = Q_1 \Sigma Q_2^T$

Detailed Example (2×2 Matrix)

Given Matrix: $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$

Step 0: Diagonalizability
  • Eigenvalue $\lambda = 2$ (repeated)
  • Only one eigenvector → Not diagonalizable → Use SVD
Step 1: Compute $A^T A$ $$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$ Step 2: Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors
  • $\lambda_1 = 4$, $\lambda_2 = 1$
  • $x_1 = [1,2]^T$, $x_2 = [2,-1]^T$
Step 3: Normalize → $Q_2$
$x_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}[1,2]^T$,   $x_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}[2,-1]^T$
$Q_2 = [x_1\ x_2]$

Step 4: Singular Values: $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{4} = 2$,   $\sigma_2 = \sqrt{1} = 1$

Step 5: Form $\Sigma$: $\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$

Step 6: Compute $Q_1$: $y_i = \dfrac{1}{\sigma_i} A x_i$ — gives orthonormal vectors → $Q_1 = [y_1\ y_2]$

Key Concepts & Interpretations

Orthogonal Matrices:

  • $Q_1^T Q_1 = I$, $Q_2^T Q_2 = I \to$ rotations/reflections
  • Eigenvector Sources:
    • $Q_1$: eigenvectors of $AA^T$
    • $Q_2$: eigenvectors of $A^T A$

Geometric Meaning: SVD = Rotation → Scaling → Rotation

Why SVD is Important

  • Works even when matrix is not diagonalizable or rectangular
  • Applications:
    • PCA (Dimensionality reduction)
    • Image compression
    • Noise filtering
    • Data science & ML

Final Quick Summary

SVD decomposes any matrix into orthogonal matrices and singular values, providing a structured representation even when eigen-decomposition fails.

Lecture 3: Positive Definiteness

Motivation: Stationary Points

  • A point is stationary if: $\partial f/\partial x = 0$, $\partial f/\partial y = 0$
  • For quadratic function: $f(x,y) = ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$ → always has a stationary point at $(0,0)$.

Classification at $(0,0)$:

  • Minimum
  • Maximum
  • Saddle Point

Determined using second-order conditions on $a, b, c$.

Positive Definiteness

  • Definition: $f(0,0) = 0$, $f(x,y) > 0\ \forall\ (x,y) \neq (0,0)$

Geometric Meaning:

  • Graph looks like a bowl (paraboloid) → origin is unique global minimum.

Conditions for 2D Quadratic Form

For: $f(x,y) = ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$

ConditionResult
$a > 0,\ ac > b^2$✔ Positive Definite (Minimum)
$a > 0,\ c > 0$Necessary but not sufficient
$ac = b^2,\ a > 0$Positive Semi-definite
$ac = b^2,\ a < 0$Negative Semi-definite
$ac < b^2$✘ Saddle Point

Key Formula: $ac - b^2$

  • If $> 0 \to$ definite
  • If $< 0 \to$ saddle

Completing the Square

$$f(x,y) = a\left(x + \frac{b}{a}y\right)^2 + \left(c - \frac{b^2}{a}\right) y^2$$ For positivity: $$a > 0, \qquad c - \frac{b^2}{a} > 0$$

Linear Algebra Connection

  • Matrix Form: $f(x,y) = v^T A v$, where $v = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}$, $A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{bmatrix}$
  • General $n$-D Form:
    $$f(v) = v^T A v = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij} x_i x_j$$

Key Insight: Positive definiteness of the function $\iff$ matrix $A$ is positive definite.

Examples (Important)

Example 1: Saddle Point

$f(x,y) = 2x^2 + 4xy + y^2$,   $a=2, b=2, c=1$
$ac = 2$, $b^2 = 4 \Rightarrow ac < b^2$ → Saddle Point

Example 2: Simple Saddle

$f(x,y) = 2xy$,   $A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$
$ac = 0$, $b^2 = 1 \Rightarrow ac < b^2$ → Saddle Point

Example 3: Counterexample

$f(x,y) = x^2 - 10xy + y^2$,   $a=1, c=1$
Test point $(1,1)$: $f(1,1) = -8 < 0$
Not positive definite — shows $a>0, c>0$ is not sufficient.

Example 4: 3D Minimum

$f(x_1,x_2,x_3) = 2x_1^2 - 2x_1x_2 + 2x_2^2 - 2x_2x_3 + 2x_3^2$
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$ Positive definite → Minimum at origin.

Final Intuition

  • Positive definite → bowl shape → minimum
  • Negative definite → upside-down bowl → maximum
  • Saddle → curved in opposite directions

One-Line Summary: A quadratic form is positive definite if it is always positive except at the origin, which happens when $a > 0$, $ac > b^2$.

Lecture 4: Positive Definite Matrices

Core Definition

A real symmetric matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is positive definite if:

  • Condition 1 (Quadratic Form): $v^T A v > 0\ \forall\ v \neq 0$
  • Condition 2 (Eigenvalues): All eigenvalues $\lambda_i > 0$

Both conditions are equivalent.

2×2 Matrix Case (Most Important)

For $A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{bmatrix}$

Positive definite conditions:

  • $a > 0$
  • $ac - b^2 > 0$

Formulae:

  • Determinant: $\det(A) = ac - b^2 = \lambda_1 \lambda_2$
  • Trace: $\text{tr}(A) = a + c = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$
Shortcut: If $a > 0$ and $\det(A) > 0$, then $A$ is positive definite.

Proof of Equivalence (Conceptual)

Part 1: $v^T A v > 0 \implies \lambda > 0$
Take eigenvector $x$: $Ax = \lambda x$.
Then $x^T A x = \lambda \|x\|^2 > 0 \implies \lambda > 0$.

Part 2: $\lambda > 0 \implies v^T A v > 0$
For $x = \sum c_i x_i$: $x^T A x = \sum c_i^2 \lambda_i > 0$

Key Properties

  • Quadratic Form: $f(v) = v^T A v \to$ minimum at origin if $A$ is positive definite.
  • Eigenvalue Interpretation: Positive definite $\iff$ all eigenvalues $> 0$.
  • Useful Tests: Determinant & trace, leading principal minors.

Examples (Important)

Example 1: Positive Definite

$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$
$a = 2 > 0$, $\det = 4-1 = 3 > 0$ → Positive definite

Example 2: Not Positive Definite

$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$
$a = 1 > 0$, $\det = 1-4 = -3 < 0$ → Not positive definite (saddle type)

Example 3: Diagonal Matrix

$A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$
Eigenvalues $= 3, 5 > 0$ → Positive definite

Final Intuition 💡

Positive definite matrix → always gives positive output.
Think of it as: "Energy is always positive" / "Bowl-shaped surface"

One-Line Summary: A symmetric matrix is positive definite if all eigenvalues are positive, or equivalently if $v^T A v > 0$ for every non-zero vector.
